# Tone Guide: AI Proof Careers

---

## Core Feeling

Readers come to this site anxious — about their job, their future, their kids' futures. Every piece of content should leave them feeling **clearer, calmer, and more in control**. Not hyped up. Not scared. Just better informed.

The tone is: a knowledgeable friend who works in tech, cares about getting it right, and gives you a straight answer without padding.

---

## The Five Principles

### 1. Comforting and supportive
- Acknowledge that AI uncertainty is real and stressful — don't dismiss it
- Never catastrophize. Never hype. Meet readers where they are
- Frame data as a tool for control, not a verdict
- "Don't panic" is a valid thing to say, explicitly, when the data supports it

### 2. Reliable and data-driven
- Every claim that can be sourced, should be
- Prefer BLS, O*NET, peer-reviewed labor economics over punditry
- When you don't know, say so — or don't say it
- Show your math when it matters (e.g., ROI calculations, college baseline)
- Honest about limitations: "this model doesn't capture X"

**Source authority hierarchy (highest to lowest):**
1. BLS, O*NET, peer-reviewed labor economics
2. Stack Overflow annual developer survey, GitHub research blog, IEEE Spectrum
3. MIT Technology Review, Wired (data-backed pieces only)
4. Major newspapers (NYT, WSJ) on labor/tech topics

**Source freshness:** Prefer 2025 or 2026. Do not cite articles older than 2024 unless there is no newer equivalent. Flag if the best available source is older than 12 months.

**Sources to avoid:** vendor blogs, career advice sites (Indeed, LinkedIn editorial, etc.), opinion pieces without data, anything from The New Stack, Forbes contributor network, or similar low-editorial-bar outlets.

### 3. Succinct
- One idea per paragraph. Short paragraphs
- No preamble. Lead with the finding, not the setup
- Cut filler: "It's worth noting that..." → just say it
- Tables and bullet points over long prose when listing comparisons
- A high schooler should never have to re-read a sentence to understand it

### 4. Understandable by high schoolers
- No jargon without a one-phrase definition the first time it appears
- Short sentences. Active voice
- Use concrete examples over abstract explanations
- Analogies are welcome when they clarify, not when they pad
- Avoid academic hedging ("it could be argued that...") — just state the point

### 5. Direct and honest
- Say the hard thing: some careers won't pay well, some paths have real barriers
- Don't oversell — "different path, not easier path"
- When two things are true and in tension, acknowledge both
- Don't write to make readers feel good about a bad choice

---

## Voice Characteristics

**Use:**
- Short declarative sentences: "The gap is real. The math is simple."
- Third person for occupation descriptions ("this role", "the job") — not second person
- Present tense where possible
- Specific numbers over vague ranges when available
- Active voice: "AI excels at pattern-matching" not "Pattern-matching tasks are excelled at by AI"

**Avoid:**
- "In today's rapidly changing world..."
- "It's important to note that..."
- "Many experts believe..."
- Rhetorical questions as section headers
- Inspirational language without data to back it up
- Hedging that sounds like a disclaimer
- Addressing the worker directly ("your tasks", "you should") — talk about the job, not the person
- Doomsday or scary framing: "AI is taking over", "replaced by AI", "wiped out", "under threat" — always use neutral or optimistic framing instead. Say what AI is doing, not what it's destroying.
- Vague AI/business-speak: "lean into", "upskill", "future-proof", "pivot to" — these are empty calories. Say what the person should actually do.

---

## Structure Pattern (for most posts)

1. **Lead with the finding** — the key data point or answer, upfront
2. **Show the evidence** — tables, numbers, comparisons
3. **Explain what it means** — implications for the reader
4. **Honest caveats** — what the data doesn't capture, who this applies to
5. **Actionable takeaway** — what should you actually do with this

## Occupation Next Steps: Risks & Opportunities

For content rules, section schema, and what to include/exclude, see `docs/career_page_spec.md`.

### Tone for risks & opportunities specifically
- Talk about the job, not the worker. "This role's core tasks" not "your tasks."
- Casual but direct. A high school student should get it on the first read.
- Specific. Name the actual task. "Observing patients and charting condition changes" not "clinical observation tasks."
- Don't moralize or inspire. State what the data shows and what it means practically.

### Examples of good vs bad

**Bad (too generic):**
> "Some tasks in this role are being automated, but there are still opportunities for human workers to add value through judgment and creativity."

**Good:**
> "Building and maintaining websites — the biggest task in this job by usage — shows up in nearly 26,000 Claude conversations, with about 40% automation patterns. AI is already handling a real chunk of routine coding."

**Bad (addresses worker directly):**
> "Your strongest move is to lean into the tasks AI isn't touching yet."

**Good:**
> "AI is starting to take on the administrative and documentation side of nursing — organizing records, automating charting, and flagging early signs of patient deterioration through remote monitoring tools."

## Audience

Always frame information to be useful for two audiences:
1. **People currently in the role** — what should they pay attention to, lean into, or start learning?
2. **People thinking of getting into the role** — is this a good bet? What version of this job is worth pursuing?

Every section should pass the "so what do I do with this?" test. Data without a practical implication is just noise.

---

## What This Is Not

- Not a tech enthusiast blog ("AI is amazing and here's why you should embrace it")
- Not a doom blog ("AI will take all our jobs")
- Not a career advice column ("follow your passion")
- Not an academic paper

It is: **data journalism for people making real decisions about their careers and their kids' futures.**

---

## Feynman Style

Write like you're explaining something interesting to a friend — not like you're giving advice.

- **State what's true, plainly.** Don't tell people what to do with it. Trust them to figure it out.
- **No jargon, no business-speak.** If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough.
- **Slightly curious, slightly irreverent.** The data is interesting. Let that come through.
- **Name the specific thing.** "39% of AI coding conversations are fully hands-off" — not "a significant portion of tasks are being automated."
- **Don't moralize.** The reader is an adult making a real decision. Give them the facts and get out of the way.
